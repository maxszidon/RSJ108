Max Szidon's Guide on How to Use a Camera in Manual Mode

An Instruction Challenge for JOUR108

How to make a well-exposed photograph using manual settings

Assembly time: 10-30 minutes
Awesome rating: 100% awesome

Using a professional camera like a digital single lens reflex (DSLR) or mirrorless camera can deliver sharp, crisp photographs with a wide dynamic range to get the most out of editing. While these new cameras have automatic modes which make shooting easier, using a camera in manual mode still gives the user full control over the photograph. Whether you are taking photos for journalistic, personal, or commercial needs, either on digital or film, manual shooting allows you to take photographs on any camera.

  • Things You'll Need

  • Camera Body
  • Lens
  • Willingness to Learn!

  1. ISO

    ISO (formerly called ASA) controls how sensitive the sensor or film is to light. Low ISO values like 100–200 are less sensitive and produce cleaner, sharper images, while high ISO values like 800–1600 increase sensitivity for low-light shooting but add more noise or grain.

    1600 ISO vs 80 ISO

  2. Aperture

    Aperture controls how much light enters the lens and affects depth of field. Aperture ranges vary by lens, but a typical range is from about f/2.8 (wide) to f/22 (narrow). Wide apertures create a shallow depth of field with a blurry background, while narrow apertures keep more of the scene in focus.

    Look at the background of each photograph. Notice the difference?

  3. Shutter Speed

    You can adjust shutter speed through your camera’s settings or an external dial. Shutter speed controls how long the sensor or film is exposed to light and strongly affects a photo’s look. Use fast speeds (e.g., 1/2000) to freeze motion, or very slow speeds (e.g., several minutes) to capture effects like star trails. Most cameras offer shutter speeds from around 1 second or slower up to 1/500 to 1/8000, depending on the model.

    Physical shutter speed dial

  4. Light Meter

    Cameras use a light meter to show whether your photo is over or under-exposed. Adjust your ISO, aperture, or shutter speed until the meter’s indicator—or, on older film cameras, the moving needle—sits in the center, signaling proper exposure.
